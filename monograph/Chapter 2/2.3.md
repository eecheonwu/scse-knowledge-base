# 2.3 Why “SSOT-Centric” Instead of “SSOT-Driven”

The terminology adopted within a scientific discipline is not merely descriptive; it reflects the underlying conceptual framework through which the discipline understands its objects of study. Throughout the history of software engineering, the introduction of new paradigms has often been accompanied by the development of new terminology that distinguishes foundational concepts from their practical implementations. Object-Oriented Software Engineering, Model-Driven Engineering, Service-Oriented Architecture, Domain-Driven Design, and DevOps all illustrate how carefully chosen terminology communicates both the scope and philosophical orientation of a proposed approach. In a similar manner, this book deliberately distinguishes between **SSOT-Centric Software Engineering (SCSE)** and **SSOT-Driven Software Engineering (SDSE)** because the two concepts operate at different levels of abstraction and serve fundamentally different purposes.

At first glance, the terms *centric* and *driven* may appear interchangeable. Both suggest that the Single Source of Truth plays an important role within software engineering. However, closer examination reveals that they embody distinct philosophical assumptions. The distinction is not simply semantic; it defines the theoretical architecture of the entire framework proposed in this book.

## 2.3.1 Understanding “Driven”

Within engineering literature, the term **driven** generally describes a methodology, process, or operational approach in which a particular artifact or principle directs specific engineering activities. Examples include **Model-Driven Engineering (MDE)**, **Test-Driven Development (TDD)**, **Behavior-Driven Development (BDD)**, and **Data-Driven Decision Making**. In each case, the adjective *driven* indicates that an engineering activity is guided by a particular input or governing artifact.

For example, in Test-Driven Development, automated tests guide software implementation. Developers write tests before implementation, allowing the tests to drive the design and verification of software components. Similarly, in Model-Driven Engineering, formal models drive code generation, system transformation, and software implementation.

The defining characteristic of “driven” methodologies is therefore procedural. They prescribe **how engineering activities should be performed**.

Applying this interpretation to software engineering suggests that **SSOT-Driven Software Engineering (SDSE)** would describe a methodology in which software development activities are guided by an authoritative Single Source of Truth. Requirements analysis, architecture design, implementation planning, task planning, code generation, testing, deployment, and software evolution would all derive directly from the engineering SSOT.

This interpretation is both useful and correct.

However, it describes only **one method of applying** the Single Source of Truth.

It does not define the broader philosophy of software engineering itself.

## 2.3.2 Understanding “Centric”

The term **centric**, by contrast, expresses a fundamentally different idea.

Rather than describing an engineering process, *centric* identifies the **central organizing principle** around which an entire discipline, framework, or system is structured.

Examples from computing illustrate this distinction clearly.

User-centric design positions user needs as the central organizing principle of product development.

Data-centric computing organizes computational systems around the management and movement of data.

Knowledge-centric organizations recognize organizational knowledge as the primary strategic asset guiding decision-making.

In each case, the adjective *centric* defines the **primary object around which all other activities revolve**.

Within this book, **SSOT-Centric Software Engineering** adopts exactly this interpretation.

Rather than proposing a methodology for building software, SCSE proposes a new way of understanding software engineering itself.

Its central proposition is straightforward:

**Engineering knowledge, preserved through an authoritative Engineering Single Source of Truth, constitutes the primary asset of software engineering.**

Every engineering activity is therefore organized around the Engineering SSOT because it represents the authoritative body of engineering knowledge governing the software system.

Requirements exist because they express engineering knowledge.

Architecture exists because it structures engineering knowledge.

Implementation exists because it realizes engineering knowledge.

Testing exists because it validates engineering knowledge.

Deployment exists because it operationalizes engineering knowledge.

Software evolution exists because engineering knowledge itself evolves.

Consequently, the Engineering SSOT is not simply an input to engineering activities.

It becomes the conceptual center of the entire software engineering discipline.

## 2.3.3 Paradigm versus Methodology

The distinction between SCSE and SDSE can therefore be understood as the distinction between a **paradigm** and a **methodology**.

A paradigm establishes the fundamental assumptions upon which a discipline is built.

A methodology specifies procedures for applying those assumptions in practice.

SCSE defines the paradigm.

SDSE defines one methodology operating within that paradigm.

This relationship mirrors established patterns within software engineering.

Object-Oriented Software Engineering introduced an object-oriented paradigm.

Within that paradigm emerged numerous methodologies, design patterns, implementation strategies, and programming languages.

Similarly, Agile Software Engineering introduced a philosophy centered on iterative development and customer collaboration.

Within Agile emerged Scrum, Kanban, Extreme Programming, Feature-Driven Development, and numerous implementation frameworks.

The paradigm provides the conceptual foundation.

Methodologies operationalize the paradigm.

Likewise, **SCSE establishes the conceptual foundation**, while **SDSE operationalizes software engineering through the Engineering Single Source of Truth**.

## 2.3.4 Why This Distinction Matters

The distinction becomes increasingly important as the scope of SCSE expands.

Throughout this book, numerous theories and engineering mechanisms are introduced, including:

·        Knowledge Translation Theory

·        Architecture Memory

·        Engineering Knowledge Ontology (EKO)

·        SSOT Knowledge Graphs

·        SSOT Translation Quality Evaluation (STQE)

·        SSOT-Driven Change Impact Analysis

·        Multi-Agent Software Engineering

·        Continuous Architecture Governance

Not all of these contributions describe software development processes.

Some define theoretical models.

Some define semantic representations.

Others define governance mechanisms, evaluation metrics, or engineering services.

Collectively, they extend well beyond the scope of a single software development methodology.

Attempting to describe this entire body of work as **SSOT-Driven Software Engineering** would therefore be conceptually restrictive because many of these contributions are not “driven” processes.

Instead, they exist because software engineering has adopted a knowledge-centric worldview in which the Engineering SSOT functions as the organizing principle.

The adjective **centric** therefore accommodates the broader research programme while preserving conceptual coherence.

## 2.3.5 Hierarchical Relationship Between SCSE and SDSE

This book defines the relationship between SCSE and SDSE as a hierarchical framework.

At the highest level is the discipline of **Software Engineering**.

Within Software Engineering, this book proposes **SSOT-Centric Software Engineering** as a new knowledge-centric paradigm.

Operating within SCSE are multiple complementary theories, models, and methodologies.

One of these methodologies is **SSOT-Driven Software Engineering (SDSE)**.

SDSE describes how engineering activities are executed using the Engineering SSOT as their authoritative operational input.

Thus:

·        **SCSE answers the question:** *What is the primary asset of software engineering?*

·        **SDSE answers the question:** *How should software engineering activities be performed using that asset?*

This distinction enables future research to introduce additional methodologies under the SCSE paradigm without altering its theoretical foundations.

For example, future work may define:

·        SSOT-Driven Requirements Engineering

·        SSOT-Driven Architecture Design

·        SSOT-Driven Testing

·        SSOT-Driven DevOps

·        SSOT-Driven Maintenance

·        SSOT-Driven AI Agent Collaboration

Each methodology would inherit the principles established by SCSE while addressing a specific engineering activity.

## 2.3.6 Formal Distinction

The distinction proposed in this book may be summarized formally.

**Definition 2.2 (SSOT-Centric Software Engineering).**

**SSOT-Centric Software Engineering (SCSE) is a software engineering paradigm that recognizes authoritative engineering knowledge, represented through an Engineering Single Source of Truth, as the central organizing principle governing the creation, translation, validation, governance, and evolution of software systems.**

**Definition 2.3 (SSOT-Driven Software Engineering).**

**SSOT-Driven Software Engineering (SDSE) is a methodology within the SCSE paradigm in which software engineering activities are systematically performed by deriving engineering decisions, artifacts, and workflows from the authoritative Engineering Single Source of Truth.**

These definitions establish a clear conceptual hierarchy.

SCSE provides the philosophical and theoretical foundation.

SDSE provides one mechanism for implementing that foundation.

## 2.3.7 Implications for the SCSE Research Programme

The distinction between SCSE and SDSE has important implications for the long-term research programme proposed in this monograph.

By defining SCSE as the overarching paradigm, the framework gains the flexibility to incorporate future theories, semantic models, engineering services, governance mechanisms, AI reasoning frameworks, and evaluation methodologies without redefining its conceptual identity. SCSE becomes the intellectual umbrella under which a family of complementary contributions can evolve while remaining anchored to the same foundational principle: **engineering knowledge, preserved within an Engineering Single Source of Truth, is the primary asset of software engineering**.

This distinction also provides a coherent structure for future scholarly work. Individual research contributions—such as SSOT-Driven Feature Evolution, Architecture Memory, Knowledge Translation Theory, Engineering Knowledge Ontology, STQE, or Autonomous Architecture Governance—can each be developed as independent theories or methodologies while contributing to a unified body of knowledge centered on SCSE. Rather than producing isolated innovations, the research programme progressively expands a single, coherent paradigm.

The remainder of this chapter builds upon this conceptual distinction by formally defining **SSOT-Centric Software Engineering**.
